4TH SEMISTER BASIC INSTRUMENTATION

1st Lesson: Basics of Measurement, Electronic Voltmeter, and Cathode Ray Oscilloscope

  • Basics of Measurement: This topic introduces the fundamental concepts of measurement. It covers various aspects, including
  • Measurement Systems: Students learn about the different types of measurement systems used in instrumentation, such as analog and digital systems, and their components.
  • Units of Measurement: The lesson explores the commonly used units of measurement, such as volts, amperes, ohms, and hertz, as well as their symbols and conversions.
  • Accuracy and Precision: The concepts of accuracy (how close a measured value is to the true value) and precision (the degree of repeatability in measurements) are explained.
  • Resolution: This refers to the smallest increment that can be detected and displayed by a measurement instrument. The concept of resolution is discussed in relation to digital instruments.
  • Calibration: The importance of calibration in maintaining the accuracy and reliability of measurement instruments is emphasized. Students learn about calibration procedures and standards.
  • Electronic Voltmeter: An electronic voltmeter is an instrument used to measure voltage. It operates based on the principle of converting the electrical quantity being measured into a proportional electrical signal. The main subtopics within this lesson can include:
  • Working Principles: Students learn about different types of voltmeters, such as analog and digital voltmeters, and their working principles. Analog voltmeters typically use a moving-coil or moving-iron mechanism, while digital voltmeters employ analog-to-digital converters (ADCs).
  • Types of Voltmeters: The lesson covers various types of voltmeters, including analog panel meters, digital multimeters (DMMs), and specialized voltmeters like true RMS voltmeters or differential voltmeters.
  • Applications: The applications of voltmeters in measuring voltage in different electrical circuits, systems, and devices are discussed. Examples include voltage measurements in power supplies, electronic circuits, and communication systems.
  • Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO): A cathode ray oscilloscope, commonly known as a CRO or oscilloscope, is a device used to visualize and analyze electrical waveforms. The main subtopics within this lesson can include:
  • Working Principles: Students learn about the basic components of a CRO, including the cathode ray tube (CRT), deflection plates, timebase generator, and vertical amplifier. The lesson explains how the electron beam is deflected to trace waveforms on the screen.
  • Waveform Visualization: This subtopic covers the display of different waveforms on the oscilloscope screen, including sine waves, square waves, triangular waves, and complex waveforms. Students learn to interpret waveform characteristics such as amplitude, frequency, and phase.
  • Timebase and Triggering: The lesson explains the timebase control and triggering functionality of the oscilloscope, which allows the user to accurately observe repetitive or single-shot waveforms. Timebase controls the horizontal sweep speed, while triggering ensures stable waveform display.
  • Applications: The applications of CROs in various fields, such as electronics, telecommunications, and research, are explored. Examples include waveform analysis, signal troubleshooting, frequency response measurement, and transient event visualization.

2nd Lesson: Signal Generators

  • Signal Generators: Signal generators are instruments used to produce electronic signals of different waveforms, frequencies, and amplitudes. They are essential tools in various fields, including electronics, telecommunications, and research. The main subtopics within this lesson can include:
  • Types of Signal Generators: This subtopic covers different types of signal generators, such as function generators, arbitrary waveform generators (AWGs), and RF signal generators. It explains the features, capabilities, and applications of each type.
  • Waveform Generation: Students learn about the generation of various waveforms, including sine waves, square waves, triangular waves, pulse waves, and more. The lesson explains the parameters involved in waveform generation, such as frequency, amplitude, duty cycle, and phase.
  • Frequency Synthesis and Modulation: This section focuses on frequency synthesis techniques used in signal generators, such as direct digital synthesis (DDS) or phase-locked loop (PLL). It also introduces modulation techniques like amplitude modulation (AM), frequency modulation (FM), and phase modulation (PM).
  • Applications of Signal Generators: Students learn about the diverse applications of signal generators, such as testing and characterization of electronic devices, research and development, communications system testing, audio signal generation, and frequency response analysis.
